What Is EPDM Roofing? A Complete Guide

EPDM (Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer) is a synthetic rubber single-ply roofing membrane used on flat and low-slope commercial and residential roofs. It is the most proven flat roofing material available — with a track record going back to the 1960s — and is known for exceptional flexibility in cold weather, excellent UV resistance, and a lifespan of 20–30 years. It is one of three single-ply systems Atlas Roofing installs, alongside TPO and PVC.

How EPDM roofing works

EPDM is a rubber membrane — typically black — installed in large sheets over roof insulation or directly over the deck. The sheets are joined at seams using tape or adhesive, and the membrane is held in place by one of three methods: fully adhered (bonded to the substrate), mechanically attached (fastened through the membrane), or ballasted (held down by gravel or pavers).

Unlike thermoplastic membranes (TPO and PVC), EPDM cannot be heat-welded — its seams rely on adhesive tape or liquid adhesive. Modern EPDM tape seam technology has improved significantly, but heat-welded seams (available on TPO and PVC) are generally stronger.

Why EPDM performs well in Northeast Ohio's climate

EPDM's rubber chemistry maintains excellent flexibility in cold temperatures — it doesn't become brittle in the freeze-thaw cycles that characterize Ohio winters. This cold-weather performance is one of the main reasons EPDM has remained competitive with newer thermoplastic systems in cold-climate markets. It also has excellent UV resistance and ozone resistance — characteristics that contribute to its long proven lifespan.

EPDM's main limitation — seam performance

The primary disadvantage of EPDM relative to TPO and PVC is seam strength. Heat-welded thermoplastic seams are stronger than the membrane itself — EPDM tape seams are not. Properly installed EPDM tape seams have a good track record, but seam failures are the primary failure mode for EPDM systems, particularly at lap edges exposed to water pooling.

When EPDM makes the most sense: Buildings with minimal HVAC penetrations (fewer seam interruptions), buildings where the long track record matters more than energy efficiency, and re-roofing projects where the existing system is EPDM in good condition and a recover or patch is warranted. For buildings prioritizing energy efficiency, white TPO is usually the better recommendation.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does EPDM roofing last?
Well-installed EPDM roofing in Northeast Ohio typically lasts 20–30 years. Lifespan depends on membrane thickness (60 mil is current standard), seam quality, drainage design, and maintenance frequency. Regular semi-annual inspections extend EPDM life by catching seam issues early.
Is EPDM roofing better than TPO?
Neither is universally better — it depends on the building. EPDM has a longer proven track record and excellent cold-weather flexibility. TPO has stronger heat-welded seams and significantly better energy efficiency (white vs. black). For most new commercial installations in Northeast Ohio, Atlas Roofing recommends TPO. EPDM is a strong choice for re-roofing existing EPDM systems or buildings where heat-weld capability is not a priority.
Can EPDM roofing be repaired?
Yes — EPDM repairs are straightforward using patch material and compatible adhesive or tape. Minor punctures, small tears, and localized seam failures can typically be repaired effectively. Widespread seam failure or membrane degradation indicates replacement is needed.
What does EPDM roofing cost per square foot?
EPDM roofing costs vary based on building size, installation method, insulation requirements, and existing roof condition. Installed cost is comparable to TPO. Atlas Roofing provides free written estimates after inspecting your commercial building.
Does EPDM work in Ohio winters?
Yes — EPDM's rubber chemistry maintains flexibility in cold temperatures, making it well-suited to Ohio's freeze-thaw climate. It's one of the reasons EPDM has remained widely used in cold-climate markets despite competition from newer thermoplastic systems.
