Does Homeowners Insurance Cover Roof Replacement in Ohio?

Homeowners insurance covers roof replacement in Ohio when the damage is caused by a sudden, accidental event covered by your policy — typically hail, wind, falling trees, and fire. It does not cover replacement due to normal wear, aging, or deferred maintenance. The type of coverage you have (Actual Cash Value vs. Replacement Cost Value) significantly affects your payout. Most Northeast Ohio homeowners with policies written in the last 10 years should have Replacement Cost Value coverage — check your declarations page.

What insurance typically covers

Standard HO-3 homeowner's insurance policies in Ohio cover roof damage from these named perils:

  • Hail: The most common claim in Northeast Ohio. Any hail event that causes sufficient damage — typically quarter-size or larger — may qualify.
  • Wind: High wind damage including missing shingles, lifted sections, and structural damage from fallen limbs or trees.
  • Fire: Including lightning strikes that cause fire damage.
  • Falling objects: Trees, limbs, and other falling debris.
  • Weight of ice, snow, or sleet: Structural damage from snow and ice load.

What insurance does NOT cover

  • Normal wear and aging: A 22-year-old roof that needs replacement because it's old is a maintenance expense, not an insured loss.
  • Neglect or deferred maintenance: Damage that resulted from failing to maintain a roof that was already in poor condition.
  • Cosmetic damage only: Some policies exclude cosmetic hail damage — dented metal but no loss of function. Read your policy carefully.
  • Workmanship defects: Poor installation by a previous contractor is not covered by homeowner's insurance (though it may be covered by a workmanship warranty).

ACV vs RCV — the most important distinction

Actual Cash Value (ACV)Replacement Cost Value (RCV)
What it paysDepreciated value of damaged roofFull cost to replace with like materials
Example: 20-year-old roofMay pay 30–50% of replacement costPays full replacement cost minus deductible
Depreciation holdbackDepreciation is final — no recoveryHoldback released after work is completed
How to confirmCheck declarations pageLook for "RCV" or "replacement cost" in policy

If your policy is ACV, an older roof may generate a very small insurance payment after depreciation. Consider discussing a policy upgrade with your insurance agent — the premium difference for RCV coverage is often modest.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Does insurance pay for a full roof replacement or just repairs?
It depends on the extent of documented damage. If hail or wind damage is widespread across the entire roof, most policies will fund full replacement. If damage is isolated to a small area, the payment may cover repair only. The adjuster's scope should match documented damage — that's why having a contractor present at the adjuster inspection matters.
What is an insurance deductible for a roof claim?
Your deductible is the amount you pay before insurance coverage kicks in. For roof claims, some policies have a separate wind/hail deductible that is a percentage of your home's insured value (e.g., 1% or 2%) rather than a flat dollar amount. Check your declarations page for your specific deductible.
Can I keep insurance money and not fix my roof?
Technically yes on an ACV policy — you receive the cash payment and can use it as you choose. On RCV policies, the depreciation holdback is only released after documented completion of the work. Not replacing a damaged roof and keeping insurance money creates ongoing risk to your home and may affect future claims.
What if my insurance company denies my roof claim?
Denied claims can be appealed with additional documentation. Common denial reasons: damage attributed to wear rather than storm, claim filed after policy deadline, or damage assessed as below deductible. A public adjuster or your roofing contractor can help prepare supplemental documentation for an appeal.
Should I file a claim for minor hail damage?
Consider carefully — filing a claim creates a record on your CLUE report, which can affect future premiums. For damage that barely exceeds your deductible, out-of-pocket repair may be more economical long-term. For significant damage that clearly warrants full replacement, filing is the right decision. A free inspection helps you understand the scope before deciding.
