What Is PVC Roofing? A Complete Commercial Flat Roof Guide

PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride) is a thermoplastic single-ply roofing membrane used on flat and low-slope commercial roofs. Like TPO, it is white and heat-welded at seams — creating stronger seams than adhesive systems. PVC's key advantage over TPO is superior chemical resistance, making it the standard choice for restaurants, industrial buildings, and any facility with rooftop grease or chemical exhaust exposure. It has been used commercially since the 1960s and has an excellent long-term track record in cold climates.

How PVC roofing works

PVC is a thermoplastic membrane — it softens when heated and can be welded to itself using hot air. This heat-welding capability is its most important feature: properly welded PVC seams are stronger than the membrane itself, creating a monolithic waterproof surface that doesn't rely on adhesives that can fail over time. PVC is installed in sheets over insulation boards, either fully adhered, mechanically fastened, or ballasted.

PVC vs TPO — the main comparison

Both PVC and TPO are white thermoplastic membranes that are heat-welded and Energy Star rated. The differences are:

When PVC is the right choice

Restaurants and food service: Grease exhaust from kitchen hoods is the primary use case. Grease degrades TPO and EPDM over time; PVC is highly resistant. Any building with significant kitchen exhaust should have PVC — not TPO — under or near exhaust equipment.

Industrial and manufacturing: Chemical exposure, fuel, or solvent vapors that might contact the roof surface are better handled by PVC.

Long track record priority: PVC has been in commercial service since the 1960s. Its long-term performance data under various conditions is extensive. For building owners who prioritize proven materials, PVC's track record is longer than TPO's.

For standard office, retail, and warehouse buildings: TPO is typically the cost-effective first choice. PVC is worth specifying when chemical or grease exposure is a factor, or when the longer track record is a priority. Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does PVC roofing last?
Well-installed PVC roofing in Northeast Ohio typically lasts 20–30 years. One consideration: PVC contains plasticizers that can leach out over time in some formulations, reducing flexibility. Modern PVC formulations address this with improved plasticizer chemistry. Lifespan depends on membrane quality, installation, and maintenance.
Is PVC roofing good for restaurants in Ohio?
Yes — PVC is the standard recommendation for restaurant roofing specifically because of its superior resistance to grease and cooking oil exposure. Grease from kitchen exhaust degrades TPO and EPDM over time; PVC maintains its performance in grease-exposure conditions.
What is the difference between PVC and TPO roofing?
Both are white thermoplastic membranes that are heat-welded. PVC has superior chemical and grease resistance, a longer track record, and slightly higher cost. TPO is the current standard for most commercial buildings without chemical exposure and generally costs less per square foot. Both offer Energy Star cool roof performance.
Can PVC be installed in Ohio winters?
Yes — modern PVC formulations maintain adequate flexibility for installation in cold weather, though very cold temperatures require additional care. Atlas Roofing installs PVC year-round on Northeast Ohio commercial buildings.
What maintenance does a PVC roof require?
Semi-annual inspections to check seams, drains, and membrane condition. PVC is relatively low-maintenance, but prompt repair of any punctures or seam issues extends system life. Debris accumulation at drains should be cleared regularly to prevent ponding.
