What Is Modified Bitumen Roofing?

Modified bitumen (mod bit) is a multi-layer asphalt-based roofing system used on flat and low-slope commercial and residential roofs. It is made from asphalt modified with polymers (APP or SBS) for improved flexibility and performance. Modified bitumen typically consists of 2–3 plies installed over insulation and a base sheet, providing excellent waterproofing, puncture resistance, and UV stability. It is one of the most durable flat roofing options and is widely used on Northeast Ohio commercial buildings.

How modified bitumen works

Modified bitumen systems are built in layers. A base sheet is adhered or mechanically attached to the roof deck or insulation. One or more cap sheets are installed over the base, lapping at edges to create a continuous waterproof assembly. The cap sheet is surfaced with granules (like asphalt shingles) or with a smooth aluminum or foil surface for UV reflection.

The modified asphalt that gives mod bit its name is asphalt combined with polymers that improve performance beyond standard built-up roofing:

  • APP-modified (Atactic Polypropylene): Applied with a torch — the installer heats the back of the sheet to fuse it to the substrate. Creates a strong bond. APP performs well in hot conditions.
  • SBS-modified (Styrene-Butadiene-Styrene): A rubber-based modifier that creates a more flexible membrane, better suited to cold climates. SBS can be cold-applied (adhesive), hot-mopped, or torch-applied. SBS is generally preferred for Northeast Ohio's climate because of its superior cold-weather flexibility.

Why modified bitumen is used in Northeast Ohio

Modified bitumen's multi-layer construction provides excellent redundancy — multiple waterproofing layers mean a single point failure doesn't immediately result in a leak. Its granule-surfaced cap is highly walkable, which matters on commercial buildings where HVAC technicians and other tradespeople access the roof regularly. SBS-modified systems maintain flexibility in Ohio's cold winters. And the 20–30 year lifespan with proper maintenance is competitive with single-ply systems.

Best suited for: Commercial buildings with regular roof access (HVAC service, mechanical equipment), buildings where redundant waterproofing layers are a priority, and re-roofing of existing mod bit systems where the substrate is sound.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does modified bitumen roofing last?
Well-installed modified bitumen roofing on Northeast Ohio commercial buildings typically lasts 20–30 years. Lifespan depends on membrane quality, installation method, drainage design, and maintenance frequency. SBS systems in our cold climate tend to outperform APP in terms of cold-weather flexibility and longevity.
What is the difference between APP and SBS modified bitumen?
APP (Atactic Polypropylene) modified bitumen is torch-applied and performs well in heat. SBS (Styrene-Butadiene-Styrene) is rubber-modified, more flexible in cold temperatures, and can be cold-applied, hot-mopped, or torched. For Northeast Ohio's climate, SBS is generally preferred due to superior cold-weather performance.
Is modified bitumen better than TPO?
Neither is universally better. Modified bitumen offers multi-layer redundancy and superior walkability. TPO offers better energy efficiency (white reflective surface) and heat-welded seams. For buildings with heavy rooftop traffic or where redundant waterproofing layers are prioritized, mod bit is a strong choice. For energy efficiency and simpler maintenance, TPO is often preferred.
Can modified bitumen be repaired?
Yes — modified bitumen repairs are straightforward using compatible patch materials and adhesive or torch. Small punctures, localized seam failures, and minor blisters can be effectively repaired. Widespread delamination or granule loss across the cap sheet indicates the system is approaching end of life.
How much does modified bitumen roofing cost?
Modified bitumen costs vary based on building size, number of plies, and existing roof condition. It is generally comparable to or slightly higher than single-ply TPO or EPDM for new installations, due to the multi-layer installation process.
